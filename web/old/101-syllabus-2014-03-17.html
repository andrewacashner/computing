Introduction to Western Art Music (Music 101)

Andrew Cashner, Lecturer (cashner at uchicago)
University of Chicago, Spring 2014

Syllabus

In this course you will learn how to listen for how musical structures have been used to create meaning and shape experience. Together we will consider how music might function as an art form, based on select examples from the European and North American tradition generally known as “Classical.”

Overview

This course provides a basic orientation for experiencing music as art within Western culture (which for this course means primarily Europe and North America). The central question for the course is this:
How have people used music as an art form to create meaning and shape experience?

This question depends on others:

  • What kind of an art is music?
  • How do musical structures create meaning, and what kind of meaning do they create?
  • How has this changed over time in different historical and cultural contexts?
  • And above all, how can we learn to hear this?

We will explore these questions by engaging closely with a wide range of music from medieval songs to modern concert music (mostly in the “Classical” category). The course is organized in thematic units: fundamental concepts and tools for listening, vocal music, instrumental music, opera and musical theater. The course is not chronological, nor is it comprehensive in any way. Rather, it focuses on developing the particular kinds of listening practice needed for distinct types of music.

The main focus, then, will be on listening. There is one required CD (see required materials below). The other music selections will be available online. The main readings are from a classic (and inexpensive) text on active listening by the renowned American composer Aaron Copland. These will be supplemented with historical primary sources and a few scholarly studies.

No prior musical experience or training is required for the course. This course fulfills the common-core requirement for an arts course.

Learning goals

  • Build skills for active, engaged, thoughtful listening
  • By encountering different kinds of musical art, question
    what art IS and what art DOES
  • Experience different ways that musical STRUCTURES can create MEANING
  • Practice articulating aesthetic experience in conversation and writing

Assignments and grading

10% Attend and participate fully in class
(be prepared, speak and listen carefully)

Late assignments will be docked a full letter grade for each day they are late, unless prior arrangements have been made with the professor.

Academic honesty

All written work must include a signed honor pledge agreeing to the following.

Students in this course must agree not to unfairly advance their own academic performance or in any way limit or impede the academic pursuits of other students. The following actions are among those the professor considers dishonest:

  1. Giving or receiving aid during any examination or otherwise cheating on an examination.
  2. Failing to distinguish carefully between one’s own work and material from any other source (e.g., written materials, oral sources, Internet or other data available through computer resources.)
  3. Misrepresenting the type or amount of work done (e.g., in falsified listening exercises).1

Penalties for academic dishonesty may range from grade deductions to a hearing before the college’s disciplinary committee.

Disabilities

We will make every effort to accommodate the needs of students with disabilities that hinder their academic performance. For this to be possible, these students must inform the professor of any special needs before the end of week one or as soon as they arise. For more information, contact the dean of students or Disabilities Services (http://disabilities.uchicago.edu).
