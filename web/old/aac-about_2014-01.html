About Andrew Cashner

I am a musician—a pianist and organist—and musicologist.
I am currently a lecturer in music and PhD candidate in the history and theory of music at the University of Chicago.

Education

  • University of Chicago, PhD candidate, History and theory of music
  • University of Notre Dame, Master of Sacred Music, Emphasis in theology and organ performance
  • New England Conservatory of Music, Graduate study, Piano performance
  • Lawrence University, Bachelor of Music, Piano performance

My principal piano teachers were Catherine Kautsky, Dmitri Novgorodsky, Alexander Korsantia, Christiane Karajeva, and Charles Combopiano, and I played in master classes for Richard Goode and Gilbert Kalish.
I studied organ with Craig Cramer and played in master classes for Jesse Eschbach and David Higgs.
I studied composition with Ethan Haimo, Daniel Pinkham, and Philippe Bodin.

Performance and Composition

I am an active pianist as well as an organist, choral conductor, and composer. I direct Quilisma, a weekly time for singing Gregorian chant at the University of Chicago.

I have worked as music director of churches in Indiana, and as a substitute organist in Chicago. I have also taught private piano, organ, and theory lessons. My recitals have integrated solo piano, organ, and harpsichord performances with chamber music and original compositions and improvisations. My compositions include concert music and church music for chorus, as well as songs and piano pieces.

Musicology Research

My dissertation is entitled “Faith, Hearing, and the Power of Music in Hispanic Villancicos, 1600–1700.” My article, “Playing Cards at the Eucharistic Table: Music, Theology, and Society in a Corpus Christi Villancico from Colonial Mexico, 1628,” is forthcoming in the Journal of Early Modern History.

My current research uses seventeenth-century villancicos (a widespread genre of vocal sacred music based on vernacular poetry) as sources for historical theological understandings of the power of music. I consider how this both popular and cultivated form of devotion fit into the evangelizing and civilizing mission of the Spanish church and state, particularly in the villancico cycles by Juan Gutiérrez de Padilla at the cathedral of Puebla de los Ángeles, Mexico. Through musical analysis I hope to discover the details of composing and performing music reflected broader concepts of music's place in the cosmos and its effect on the human body through the senses and affections. I ground my interpretations in early modern music-theoretical and theological literature and visual art.
