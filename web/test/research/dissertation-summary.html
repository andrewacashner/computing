Faith, Hearing, and the Power of Music in Hispanic Villancicos, 1600–1700

Andrew A. Cashner

PhD Dissertation, Department of Music, University of Chicago, June 2015

Central question

  • In the understanding of seventeenth-century Hispanic Catholics, what was music's role in the relationship between hearing and faith?
  • How can we explore this question from music about music in the vernacular devotional genre of villancicos?

Part I: Hearing Faith through Music in Villancicos ↑

Chapter 1: Villancicos as Musical Theology ↑

  • How did people perform their beliefs about music through the medium of music itself?
  • What are the different types of metamusical villancicos?

Sections:

  • Topics of birdsong, dance, instruments, solmization, villancicos about villancicos, angelic music

Key examples:

  • Antonio de Salazar, Angélicos coros con gozo cantad (Mexico City, ca. 1700)
  • Juan Hidalgo, Cuando el Alba aplaude alguno (Madrid, ca. 1650)

Chapter 2: Music's Power in the Relationship between Hearing and Faith ↑

  • How did Catholics use villancicos and other music to make faith appeal to hearing?
  • How did they express and respond to the tension between accommodating the sense of hearing and training it?
  • What was required to be able to hear Faith with faith?

Sections:

  1. The Challenge of Making Faith Appeal to Hearing
    • Sources: The Tridentine Catechism and related literature on making faith appeal to hearing,
    • Athanasius Kircher on the power of music,
    • Calderón's Corpus Christi play El nuevo palacio del Retiro on the weakness (and therefore virtue) of hearing and the incapacity of some to believe
  2. Villancicos on Sensation and Faith
    • Examples: Settings of Si los sentidos queja forman del pan divino (a contest of the senses) by Miguel de Irízar and Jerónimo de Carrión of Segovia,
    • Villancicos on sensory confusion (including synesthesia) and deprivation,
    • Villancicos of the Deaf Men by Juan Gutíerrez de Padilla and Matías Ruiz
  3. Listening for Unhearable Music: The Power of Music in the Neoplatonic Tradition
    • Music as a way of hearing the book of nature read aloud
    • Sources: Augustine, Boethius, Fray Luis de Granada, Introducción del símbolo de la fe, Kircher, Musurgia universalis
  4. Three Theological Functions of Villancicos: Mnemonic, Contemplative, Affective

Part II: Singing about Singing: Music as a Reflection of Heaven and an Expression of Humanity ↑

  • How did villancicos on singing and heavenly music develop over the course of the seventeenth century?
  • Did they respond to changing notions of cosmology from the new science?
  • Tracing a shift from emphasizing earthly music's similarity to heavenly music (reflection) to emphasizing the imperfection of earthly music.
  • At the same time, an increased emphasis on music's power to express and incite human affections (expression).
  • Metamusical villancicos allowed composers to demonstrate their craft and situate themselves in lineages of influence and homage.
  • Tracing multiple settings of similar poetic texts by related composers within networks of exchange.

Chapter 3: Voice and Incarnation in Padilla, Voces, las de la capilla (Puebla, 1657) ↑

  • In-depth philological analysis of complex metamusical poem and its setting, in context of theological literature available to the composer in Puebla libraries
  • How this villancico configures classic tropes of Christmas developed in Christmas liturgy, preaching, and exegetical literature
  • Emphasis on how the voices of human musicians echo the voice of Christ

Chapter 4: Heavenly Dissonance in Cererols, Suspended, cielos (Montserrat, ca. 1660) ↑

  • Trope of Christ as Verbum infans, unspeaking/infant Word whose cries are music, and become the cantus firmus of the new song of a renewed creation
  • By contrast, worldly music (including the music of the spheres) is out of tune
  • Evoking higher music through polystylistic technique and through creating moments of musical paradox

Chapter 5: The Human Side of Heavenly Music in Segovia (Irízar, Carrión, 1671–1720) ↑

  • Economy of musical production to meet local devotional needs
  • Irízar: musical scores written on backsides of received letters in makeshift notebooks, allows combined study of Irízar's correspondence with other composers about the exchange of villancico poems, and Irízar's compositional process in preparing music for Christmas 1678
  • Comparison of Irízar, Qué música celestial (heavenly music transposed to earth), and Carrión, Que destemplada armonía (untempered human music rising to heaven)
  • Connecting devotional and economic goals

Chapter 6: The Limits of Imitation in Zaragoza (Bruna, Ambiela, J. de Cáseda, to ca. 1700) ↑

  • Pieces that show the pressure to emulate past models while also innovating within the metamusical villancico tradition
  • Two settings of same textual tradition, Suban las voces al cielo by Pablo Bruna and then Miguel Ambiela (e.g., Bruna sets mild flats in flats; Ambiela, in all sharps)
  • Understanding these pieces as forms of offering in Eucharistic and Marian sanctoral devotion
  • Bruna: Connections to symbolism of the phoenix and the burning soul in emblem books and mystical literature (St. John of the Cross)
  • Representing Christ as a Vihuela in José de Cáseda, Qué música divina (in convent collection from Puebla, ca. 1700)
  • How to represent false music? (false ficta, deliberately out-of-tune music)

Part III: The Power of Music in Building a Colonial City ↑

Chapter 7: Gathering Everyone to Christ's Stable: Social Meanings of Rhythmic Movement in Puebla de los Ángeles ↑

  • The Christmas cycles of Juan Gutíerrez de Padilla (extant 1651–1659) for the Cathedral of Puebla, consecrated 1649 by Bishop Juan de Palafox y Mendoza
  • Connections with the visual art and architecture of the new high altar with paintings by Pedro García Ferrer
  • Utopian vision of colonial society, uniting diverse population around the church within a hierarchical social structure
  • Villancico cycles as microcosm of colonial society (intersection of musical and social Neoplatonism, musica instrumentalis and musica humana)
  • Rhythm as way of disciplining the body in society
  • Focus on ensaladilla, Al establo más dichoso (1652)
  • Four low-level social groups represented, each with a distinct rhythmic movement that situates them in their proper place in society
  • Last section is negrilla or black villancico, denigrating representation of slaves from Angola intended to be comic
  • Moment of paradox when the slaves say they want to sing like the angels and are joined by angelic chorus singing Gloria
  • Slaves sing in triple meter in style of villancico with colored mensural notation; angels sing in duple meter white notation quoting plainchant
  • Lowest and highest beings brought together, but also kept apart, in a utopian vision that is also hierarchical
