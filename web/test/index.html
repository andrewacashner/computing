Profile

I am a musician and scholar of music, specializing in devotional music of early modern Mexico and Spain. I am an assistant professor of musicology at the University of Southern California. In the USC Thornton School of Music, I teach courses in the history, structure, and cultural meanings of music, as of fall 2015. See the teaching page for current courses.

My research examines how people have used music to express and enact their religious beliefs. I am interested in how people across time and in different cultures use music to create relationships with each other and with what Olivier Messiaen called the world beyond, especially the ways that music can delineate and transgress boundaries between sacred and secular domains, and between elite and common social strata.

My current book project is a study of music about music in villancicos, a genre of vernacular devotional music from seventeenth-century Mexico and Spain. These metamusical villancicos—such as pieces about heavenly and angelic music, the harmony of the spheres, and representations of human singing and dancing—reveal how Hispanic Catholics understood music's power in the relationship between hearing and faith. See the research page for more information.

I perform regularly on piano, organ, and harpsichord. I enjoy collaboration and improvisation across a broad stylistic range. My compositions include choral music, songs, and solo piano pieces. See the performance page for recordings and scores.

I received the PhD from the University of Chicago in 2015, with the dissertation Faith, Hearing, and the Power of Music in Hispanic Villancicos, 1660–1700. I received the Master of Sacred Music degree from the University of Notre Dame in 2009, and a Bachelor of Music from Lawrence University in 2003. I also studied at the New England Conservatory of Music. See the curriculum vitae page for more information on my publications and experience.
