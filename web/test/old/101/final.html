Music 101 Final Project

University of Chicago, Spring 2013 (Cashner)

Overview

In collaboration with other students, research and present a profile on a composer active in the last fifty years. The groups and composers will be assigned below. Each group will prepare the following items. Details for each item are listed below.

  • A simple web page, with a link to a YouTube playlist
  • An annotated bibliography, included as part of the web page
  • A ten-minute class presentation
  • A brief report on what each member of the group contributed to the project (include this at the bottom of the web page)

Class presentations will be on Monday, June 2, and Wednesday, June 4, 2014. See specific dates for each group below.

The final web page, including the bibliography and description of the group members' work, is due on Wednesday, June 11, at 10:30 a.m. or before. This will be a polished version of the page you turned in on May 31.

The Annotated Bibliography

An annotated bibliography is a list of sources with a paragraph describing each source. The description should include a basic overview of what the source contains and what it contributes to understanding your composer. The bibliography must include the following items at a minimum. No item may count in two categories.

  • One encyclopedia article
    • Start with Grove Music Online.
    • Wikipedia doesn’t count here.
  • Two printed books
    • Literature about music is in the library (third floor) under ML.
    • There may not be a book specifically about your composer, but their works might be discussed in a book on another topic.
    • Doctoral dissertations may be used here, though published books are preferred.
  • Three articles in academic journals
    • One must date from after 2001.
  • One websites or online resource
  • Three printed musical scores
    • In the library under M.
    • These should be different genres and instrumentations (so, not all piano pieces, for example). They should reflect the composer’s career and reputation: if the composer was know for his operas, you should include an opera.
  • Five recordings
    • In the library recordings collection (third floor): ask at the desk.
    • Two of these must be vinyl LP records.
    • Two others must be compact discs.
    • One can be an online source that can’t be found in another medium, such as a live concert recording (Naxos online doesn’t count here).

The Web Page

The web page should follow this model, but with more complete information and more thoughtful analysis. See the HTML Basics tutorial to learn the basic HTML required for this assignment.

The web page should include a profile describing the composer’s place in history and the defining characteristics of the composer’s music. It should also include a concise biography in outline form, and your annotated bibliography.

The web page should include a link to a YouTube playlist with at least five videos of music by the composer. One of the members of your group will need a free Gmail account in order to log in to YouTube and create the playlist. You could also create a group Gmail account just for the purposes of this assignment.

The Class Presentation

In the class presentation, the group members will share their web page with the class and provide a basic introduction to the composer. You will not discuss your whole bibliography, though you might mention a few interesting sources. Your presentation, like the profile on the web page, should sketch an answer to these questions:

  • What did this composer see as the function of music?
  • What kinds of musical structures did the composer use?
  • What kind of meaning have people seen in the composer’s music?
  • How is the composer connected to other composers in links of influence?

Leave time to play an excerpt from one recording or video. LP records are encouraged.

Finding and Using Sources

  • U of C library catalog
  • Library’s music resources page
  • Resources available by searching the library catalog:
    • RILM, the index to literature about music, including articles, books, and dissertations
    • IIMP, the index to music periodicals (journals)
    • JSTOR, an index to journals in all subjects, with many articles available online
    • ProQuest Dissertations
    • WorldCat, a catalog of every book in every major library of the world: if you can’t find it in the Regenstein, look here and request it through Interlibrary Loan
    • Oxford Music Online, including Grove Music Online
  • Google (Google Books, Scholar, etc.)
  • Wikipedia (can be useful as a starting point)

For preparing your citations, please see the Chicago Manual of Style, 16th ed. (search the library catalog for the online version). Use the “bibliography” style for your citations. The book includes a “Quick Citation Guide” that summarizes the basic guidelines.

For questions about using these resources, ask your professor and ask the Regenstein Library’s Bibliographer for Music, Scott Landvatter (slandvat at uchicago.edu, Regenstein rm. 362).
