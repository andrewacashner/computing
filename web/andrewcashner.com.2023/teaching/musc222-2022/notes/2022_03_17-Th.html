Music in communities of belief: Roman Catholic

  • In Europe: Reform movements, conflict with Protestants
    • Use music to demonstrate power of the church and make the gospel attractive
      • “Gospel” = connection to Christ by belonging to the church
    • Traditional genres of Latin liturgical music continue
      • plainchant, faburden and other kinds of improvised parallel/simple polyphony
      • imitative solemn polyphony a la Palestrina
    • New styles develop, many in tandem with theatrical music
      • monody/recitative in sacred concertos
      • polychoral motets
      • in 18th century, concerted masses and motets (multiple movements with recitatives, arias, and choruses)

Roman Catholic, continued

  • Outside Europe:
    • “Outer circle” where Europeans were in minority (China, Japan, South Asia)
      • Jesuit missions with small followings, attempts at cultural accommodation and fusion, many unsuccessful or with unintended outcomes
      • Use music (especially speculative music theory) to find common ground with learned elite (China)
    • “Inner circle” where Europeans were a dominating force (Spain in Mexico, Peru, Paraguay/Bolivia, Philippines; Portuguese in Angola, Brazil; French in Canada, Louisiana, Haiti)
      • Colonization and church-building went hand in hand (“building a Christian civilization”)
      • Dispossession of indigenous peoples’ land base and forced relocation into missionary settlements
      • Enslavement and trafficking of African people (as well as Native American and Asian people)
      • Use music to build Catholic civilization, focus on cathedrals, monastic communities/convents
      • Chaotic and colorful contestations of cultures in public processions and festivals, e.g., Corpus Christi

Music and Catholic worldview

  • Cosmic harmony: music on earth reflects and enacts the order of creation, can bring harmony to human body and align everything with the music of the spheres and with God’s own nature
    • Boethius: music instrumentalis (played/sounding music), musica humana (body/soul, society), musica mundana (spheres, natural world)
    • Augustine: listening to earthly music but straining to hear a higher spiritual music; everything points beyond itself to God
    • God works through material elements: bread, wine, bodies of saints, special places (shrines, churches), musical instruments
  • God works through hierarchy: Christ, Mary, saints, pope, bishops, priests, down to common people; everyone has a divinely ordained place in that hierarchy, including enslaved people (at the bottom)
    • used to justify slavery, genocide, abuse
  • God speaks through tradition to the whole community (via hierarchy): must preserve old forms like chant while grafting new forms like galant orchestra music on top of them
    • The congregation does not speak for itself; the choir (including instruments like organ) speaks for it most of the time

  • Music used to teach doctrine but more to cultivate affective devotion (heartfelt experience of God and commitment to the church)
  • Special place of hearing as the sense most favored by faith, but also the weakest and most easily deceived: spiritual “ear training” necessary
  • Music is an offering to God; many people paid for music or performed music as a form of penance (in hopes of earning forgiveness of sins) or supplication (praying for rain, relief from plague, end to war); especially via music devoted to Mary and the saints

Protestant communities: Lutherans

  • (“right wing”) Lutheran
    • saved by grace alone, by faith alone, through Christ alone, as taught by Scripture alone
    • what is not forbidden may be permitted
    • music promotes the advancement of the Word
    • commitment to teaching in church, home, and school
    • Musical forms:
      • chorales/hymns and chorale-based instrumental and choral music
      • Spruchmotette, sacred concertos
      • cantatas, passions
    • music as declaration of personal faith together with rest of believing community
    • music as form of doctrinal teaching, connection to preaching
    • “New Piety” movement in 17C advocating intense personal, emotional relationship with Jesus rather than just pure doctrinal belief (partly response to 30 Ys. War, 1618–48)
    • examples: Schütz (complex choral and solo music), Crüger (simple hymns reinforced with choral arrangements), Weckmann and Buxtehude (preaching from the organ)

Other Protestants

  • (“left wing”) Reformed/Calvinist/Presbyterian
    • like Lutherans but more radical, stricter
    • predestination, election, self-policing community, in or out
    • what is not commanded must be forbiddent
    • only vocal music, metrical psalms in church
  • (“middle way”) Anglican/Episcopalian
    • compromise tradition
    • mostly like Catholicism but in English
    • parish churches more like the Reformed with psalm singing
    • Musical forms:
      • Anglican chant, anthems, organ voluntaries
  • (“radical left”) (Ana-)Baptist, Quakers
    • radical freedom and anti-traditional, all the way to absolute silence, no ceremony for the Quakers

J. S. Bach (1685–1750)

  • Musical family
  • Career:
    • Arnstadt: hotshot Lutheran organist
    • Cöthen: Calvinist, so focused on instrumental music (Brandenburg Concertos)
    • Leipzig: Kantor (= choir director) for three churches in rotation, teacher in Latin school
  • Output at Leipzig:
    • Cantatas for every week of the year, at least three cycles (many lost)
    • Passions, concerted motets (e.g., Magnificat)
  • Style and outlook
    • Conservative contrapuntal tradition (Palestrina) + Vivaldi + stirrings of galant theatrical music
    • Music as testament of faith and monument to posterity
    • Teaching, Übung, edification, personal devotion/exercise/discipline
  • Passions:
    • choruses, recit/aria pairs, chorales
    • action and reflection
