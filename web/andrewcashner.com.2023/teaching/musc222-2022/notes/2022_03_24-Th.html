Keyboard Music from Church and Home to Concert Hall

Andrew A. Cashner, PhD

Th 2022/03/24, MUSC222 Week 10

From home to concert hall

Predecessors and relatives

  • Royal and noble court music
    • Private/court opera
  • Domestic entertainment and functional music
    • lullabies, folk songs
    • “catches”, rounds, madrigals
  • Domestic religious devotion
    • Lutherans: hymn singing, chorale-based arrangements with instruments
    • Calvinists: only place for polyphonic music, but still emphasis on psalms; also polyphonic organ music “before the service” (Sweelinck)
  • Buxtehude and Abendmusik (evening music) events in Lübeck (north Germany)

Access and representation

  • The domestic sphere was an important arena for music-making by women and children
  • When domestic genres became public (i.e., moved from home to concert hall), access was generally restricted to men
  • Continued importance of amateur music-making both for creation and reception of music

Community influence on conventions

  • Commerce drives conventions
  • Public audience drives conventions
  • Conventions make communication possible within a community

Functions of keyboard music

Instruments

Large pipe organ, small organs, harpsichord (= cembalo), clavichord, fortepiano, pianoforte

Purposes

  • In church: Transitions and flow (“Preluding”), chorale/hymn/psalm introductions, versets (alternating with chorus)
    • Virtuosity in composition or performance not opposed to spiritual value
    • Improvisation was and is the primary form of keyboard music in Christian churches
      • Most music written down was designed for teaching or to leave a legacy/testament
    • Organ music was primary place to experience big, impressive, loud musical experience and larger-scale instrumental structures through mid-18C
  • At home:
    • Übung (exercise, study, practice; with spiritual connotations)
    • Edification
    • Entertainment
    • Self-expression (more emphasized in 19C)

Genres of keyboard music

  • Church
    • Praeludium (17C north German, Buxtehude)
    • Prelude and fugue (18C German, Bach)
    • Chorale prelude (17-18C Lutheran)
    • Versets (17-18C Catholic & Lutheran)
  • Home (later concert hall)
    • Suite (17-18C everywhere, especially French)
    • Partita, early sonata (early–mid 18C)
    • Sonata (mid 18C–present)

Examples

  • Suites by Johann Jakob Froberger and Elisabeth Jacquet de la Guerre
    • Froberger: Lament for Ferdinand IV (1657)
    • Jacquet de la Guerre: Suite in D (1687)
  • Preludes and fugues by J. S. Bach (Book I, 1722; Book II, 1742)
