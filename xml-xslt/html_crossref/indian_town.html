Forgetting about Indian Town: Historical Fictions of a University’s Indigenous Past

Andrew A. Cashner

At the entrance to the University of Rochester’s main campus, there used to be a historical marker with the heading INDIAN TOWN (figure). The legend explained: IN PRIMITIVE WILDERNESS HERE WAS A LARGE ALGONKIN VILLAGE WHOSE BARK CABINS AND TILLED FIELDS COVERED NINE ACRES. For those who stopped to read it the sign could evoke mystery. The legend invited readers to re-envision this western New York campus around them, erasing its quadrangles of brick Georgian Revival buildings, its famed optics research laboratories, its Greek temple of a library with its massive carillon tower—imagining this land on a bend in the Genesee River as primitive wilderness populated by some ancient people, the Algonkins. But what happened to these people or their village such that this sign was all that remained to remember them?
